This fictional scenario shows how adaptive software could respond to change without diagnosing, prescribing or acting outside configured treatment boundaries.

Maya’s fictional eight-week program

Week 1

Everything begins with momentum.

Maya starts treatment consistently. Sessions are completed on time, difficulty feels appropriate and fatigue remains manageable.

Week 3

The pattern begins to change.

Sessions start later. Completion falls. No single event proves disengagement, but the pattern no longer looks like Maya’s baseline.

Week 5

Fatigue rises. Difficulty feels heavier.

Bazi interprets the combination of delay, lower completion and reported fatigue as possible treatment friction—not as a diagnosis or judgment about effort.

Week 6

Treatment responds without losing its boundaries.

A shorter session is recommended from a provider-approved set of actions. The care team remains responsible for review and oversight.

What Bazi is designed to preserve

Adaptation should not mean surrendering control.

Bazi is being designed around constrained action, transparent reasoning and appropriate human authority.

01

Provider-defined boundaries

The system evaluates only actions configured by the therapeutic organization, provider or clinical team.

02

Human review where required

Recommendations can remain subject to review rather than being delivered as unrestricted autonomous treatment changes.

03

No diagnosis by implication

Behavioral change is evidence for interpretation, not automatic proof of a condition, motive or lack of effort.

A different philosophy of software

Software should adapt to people. Not the other way around.

The goal is not unlimited personalization. It is responsible responsiveness: noticing change, respecting boundaries and helping care remain connected to the person.

What Bazi does not claim

Responsibility matters as much as intelligence.

Bazi is presented as an emerging product concept. It has not established clinical efficacy and is not intended to replace clinicians, diagnose conditions or independently prescribe treatment.

01 / NOT A DIAGNOSTIC TOOL

Behavioral signals are not a diagnosis.

Changes in completion or timing can have many explanations and should not be interpreted as proof of a medical condition.

02 / NOT AN INDEPENDENT PRESCRIBER

Actions remain constrained.

Bazi is designed to evaluate only interventions configured by authorized therapeutic teams.

03 / FICTIONAL DEMONSTRATIONS

Maya is not a real patient.

The patient journey and product screens on this page use fictional information to demonstrate a possible future workflow.
