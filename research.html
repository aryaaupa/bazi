Bazi Research / Notes on change, care and judgment

The patient
is always
in motion.

Bazi begins with an old philosophical problem: how should a system act when the subject before it is no longer the same as the subject it first encountered?

Prologue / The premise

Medicine has always lived between constancy and change. A treatment is designed with intention, yet the person receiving it changes from hour to hour.

Energy changes. Attention changes. Symptoms change. Confidence changes. The same instruction can feel possible on Monday and impossible on Thursday.

Bazi asks whether therapeutic software can acknowledge this movement without pretending to replace human judgment.

I Motion
“All things are in motion and nothing at rest.”
Plato, Cratylus, on Heraclitus Read the text ↗

A patient is not a fixed input.

Heraclitus is remembered through the image of the river: neither the water nor the person entering it remains perfectly unchanged.

Yet much therapeutic software still assumes stability. A sequence is created, a cadence is selected and the same pathway continues even when the person’s behavior begins to communicate that something has shifted.

Bazi treats behavioral change not as noise to be ignored, but as information that may deserve interpretation.

The first proposition

A therapeutic system that can observe change but cannot respond to it is not yet adaptive.

II Meaning
“Define the measure of value.”
Strauss et al., Meaningful engagement in digital therapeutics Read the paper ↗

Engagement is not obedience.

Completion is easy to count. Meaning is harder. A person can open a program, tap through a session and produce a perfect usage metric without receiving meaningful therapeutic value.

Strauss and colleagues propose that digital therapeutics first define what value means, then operationalize meaningful engagement, implement interventions and evaluate their effect.

This distinction is central to Bazi. The purpose of adaptation is not to pressure a patient into generating more activity. It is to ask whether the treatment experience is still creating value for the person.

III Judgment
“The universe is transformation: life is opinion.”
Marcus Aurelius, Meditations IV Read the text ↗

Observation is not understanding.

Software can observe that a session was delayed. It cannot assume that it understands why. Fatigue, pain, work, caregiving, fear, confusion and ordinary human variation can produce similar digital traces.

This is where adaptive systems require restraint. A model may estimate risk, but estimation is not certainty. A signal may justify a response, but not every response should be automated.

Bazi is therefore conceived as a constrained decision layer: it evaluates provider-approved options rather than inventing treatment, and it can defer to human review when the limits of machine judgment are reached.

IV Limits
“The reason which governs knows what it does.”
Marcus Aurelius, Meditations VI Read the text ↗

Intelligence in medicine must know its boundary.

Medical software is not defined by sophistication alone. Its intended use, the decisions it informs and the risk attached to those decisions shape how it must be evaluated.

The FDA describes Software as a Medical Device as software intended for one or more medical purposes without being part of a hardware medical device.

Bazi’s eventual regulatory path will therefore depend on what the system is claimed to do, how it is implemented, the clinical context in which it is used and the degree of human oversight preserved.

Research begins where certainty ends. These are not details hidden beneath the product. They are among the central questions the product must earn the right to answer.

This requires longitudinal evidence connecting behavioral trajectories to later disengagement, while accounting for treatment type, patient context and ordinary day-to-day variation.

A responsible system should evaluate persistence, deviation from individual baseline, interaction among signals and the cost of acting too early versus too late.

The relevant endpoint cannot be app use alone. Adaptation must be evaluated against therapeutic value, adherence quality, patient burden and clinical outcomes.

The answer depends on intervention risk, reversibility, uncertainty, intended use and the oversight requirements defined by the therapeutic organization.

Explanation should be proportionate, understandable and honest about uncertainty without overwhelming the patient or falsely implying diagnostic certainty.

This remains an open validation question involving analytical performance, human factors, clinical evidence, safety monitoring and regulatory classification.
